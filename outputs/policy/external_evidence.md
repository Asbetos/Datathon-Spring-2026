# External Evidence Synthesis for Policy Brief

## 1. Federal Date Labeling Legislation

**Food Date Labeling Act of 2025** (S.2541 / H.R.4987, 119th Congress): Bipartisan bill introduced by Sen. Blumenthal (D-CT), Sen. Scott (R-FL), Rep. Pingree (D-ME), and Rep. Newhouse (R-WA). Would mandate two standardized labels: "BEST if Used By" (quality) and "USE By" (safety). Supported by 30+ companies/organizations including the Zero Food Waste Coalition and Consumer Brands Association. First time a Republican Senate co-sponsor has joined, signaling growing bipartisan momentum.
*Source: Congress.gov S.2541; BioCycle, 2025*

## 2. California AB 660 — State Date Label Model

California signed AB 660 on September 28, 2024 — the nation's first mandatory food date labeling law. Effective July 1, 2026, it bans "Sell By" dates and all ambiguous phrases (50+ variants currently in use), requiring only "BEST if Used By" (quality) or "USE By" (safety). ReFED estimates AB 660 will prevent 70,000 tons of food waste annually in California alone, saving consumers $300 million/year. Provides a state-level model for federal legislation.
*Source: NRDC; ReFED; CDFA*

## 3. California SB 1383 — Organic Waste Ban Implementation

SB 1383 (effective 2022) targets 75% reduction in organic waste disposal and 20% increase in edible food recovery by 2025. As of 2024, 75% of jurisdictions (464/616) report residential organics collection. Food rescue reached 203,000 tons in 2022 (87% of the 231,000-ton target). Agromin composted 1.25M tons in 2025 (up 250K from 2024), though <5% was food scraps. Implementation is uneven: rural areas face infrastructure gaps, and the state is not on pace for all deadlines.
*Source: Waste Dive; CalRecycle; BioCycle*

## 4. Vermont Act 148 — Universal Recycling Law

Vermont's Act 148 (passed 2012, food scrap ban effective July 2020) achieved a 50.7%–56.8% food waste recovery rate by 2023 — believed to be the highest in the U.S. Food waste tonnage in the waste stream decreased 13%. Household composting participation: 43% backyard, 12% curbside, 13% drop-off. Food donations increased nearly 40% from 2015–2016 after initial implementation phases.
*Source: Vermont DEC; BioCycle; NRDC*

## 5. Massachusetts — The Gold Standard for Waste Bans

A UC San Diego study published in *Science* (September 2024) found that of the first five states to implement food waste bans, **only Massachusetts** successfully reduced landfilled waste — achieving a 13.2% reduction. The other four states (CA, CT, RI, VT) showed no significant reduction. Key success factors: (1) most extensive network of food waste processing facilities, (2) fewest exemptions in the law. Economic impact: 1,670+ jobs created, $194M in economic value, $390M in industry activity over the ban's first decade. Diversion grew from 190,000 tons (2016) to 350,000 tons (2024). Facilities participating grew from 1,350 to 4,150.
*Source: UC San Diego/Rady School of Management (published in Science); Mass.gov; ReFED; WBUR*

## 6. UCSD Study — Bans Alone Are Insufficient

The UCSD study's central finding directly validates our DiD analysis: **organic waste bans alone do not reduce landfill waste without adequate processing infrastructure.** The expected 10% reduction from bans was not achieved in most states. The study recommends pairing mandates with infrastructure investment — exactly the policy design our three-tier framework proposes.
*Source: UC San Diego, Science (2024)*

## 7. France — International Donation Mandate Model

France's 2016 law banning supermarkets (>400 sq meters) from discarding unsold food achieved: 20%+ increase in food bank donations, 10 million additional meals/year, 46,000 tons rescued annually from 2,700+ participating stores. Quality of donations improved (more fresh foods). However, retail represents only 14% of France's food waste — household and agriculture sectors remain unaddressed. Lesson: donation mandates work for retail but must be part of a broader strategy.
*Source: PBS NewsHour; NPR; Zero Waste Europe*

## 8. Federal 2030 Goal — Off Track

The USDA/EPA 2030 goal (set 2015) aims to reduce food waste 50% from a 2016 baseline of 328 lbs/person. By 2019, per capita food waste had *increased* to 349 lbs/person — a 6% rise, moving in the wrong direction. Voluntary approaches and the "2030 Champions" program (45+ corporations) have not produced measurable aggregate reductions. This underscores the need for mandatory policy interventions.
*Source: EPA; USDA*

## 9. Federal Tax Incentives for Donation

The PATH Act (2015) extended enhanced tax deductions for food donations to all business types (not just C-corps). Businesses may deduct up to 15% of taxable income for food donations. The Food Donation Improvement Act (2022) expanded liability protections under the Bill Emerson Good Samaritan Act. In 2024, 29 states introduced 100 food waste bills; only 18% passed. New 2025 bills: Local Food Recycling and Regenerative Opportunities Act (tax incentives for households), Reduce Food Loss and Waste Act of 2025.
*Source: ReFED Policy Finder; Cherry Bekaert; Congress.gov*

## 10. ReFED Cost-Benefit Analysis

ReFED's Roadmap models 42 food waste solutions. Key findings: $18B annual investment would yield $74B in net economic benefits (4:1 return), reduce 109M MT CO2e, save 6 trillion gallons of water, recover 4.3 billion meals, and create 60,000 jobs over 10 years. Standardized date labeling is identified as the single most cost-effective solution: 425,000 tons diverted/year, $1.82B net benefit, 2M MT CO2e reduced.
*Source: ReFED Roadmap; ReFED Solutions Database*

## 11. Academic Precedent for DiD in Food Waste Policy

DiD methodology is the gold standard for policy evaluation (Callaway & Sant'Anna 2021). Applications in food waste policy remain sparse — an Italian study evaluated donation law reforms, and a Chinese quasi-experiment assessed date label interventions. A Frontiers in Sustainable Food Systems review (2023) called for "more scientific reflection on evaluation designs that can best help identify causal changes" in food waste behavior. Our analysis addresses this gap directly.
*Source: Frontiers in Sustainable Food Systems (2023); Journal of Environmental Economics & Management (2022); Callaway & Sant'Anna (2021)*
