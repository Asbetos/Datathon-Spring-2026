# Key Findings from National EDA

## Finding 1: The Residential Sector Is the #1 Policy Target
23.5M tons surplus ($141B value), 11.2M tons to landfill, **ZERO donations**.
This single sector accounts for 33% of all surplus and 49% of all landfill waste.
No other sector combines such massive volume with complete absence of diversion infrastructure.

## Finding 2: Landfill Dominates All Waste Destinations
23.0M tons (38.5% of waste) goes to landfill — the single largest destination.
Composting handles 11.5M tons (19.3%), animal feed 8.0M tons (13.4%).
Donations are just 1.78M tons (2.5% of surplus). The diversion infrastructure gap is massive.

## Finding 3: Foodservice Is the Hidden Landfill Giant
Foodservice sends 9.7M tons to landfill (42% of all landfill waste) despite being only 18% of surplus.
At $157B in surplus value, it has the highest dollar-per-ton ratio of any sector.
Plate waste (8.7M tons) is its dominant cause — behavior-change interventions needed.

## Finding 4: The Donation System Barely Exists
Only 1.78M tons donated out of 70.7M tons surplus = 2.5% donation rate.
Retail leads with 24% donation rate (896K tons), but Residential donates literally zero.
Foodservice donates only 0.87% of its uneaten food. The infrastructure for redistribution is vestigial.

## Finding 5: Top Causes Are Structural, Not Behavioral
Byproducts & production waste (11.9M tons), plate waste (8.7M tons), and spoilage (7.1M tons)
dominate causes. These are systemic issues requiring infrastructure solutions, not just awareness campaigns.
Date label concerns appear in the top 15 — targetable via federal standardization legislation.

## Finding 6: COVID-19 Caused a 24.4% Surplus Drop — Then Recovery
Surplus plunged from 74.6M tons (2019) to 56.4M tons (2020), a -24.4% shock.
Recovery of +10.4% in 2021 (62.3M tons) shows the food system's resilience.
By 2024, surplus (70.7M tons) has nearly returned to pre-pandemic levels, suggesting structural drivers persist.

## Finding 7: 221M MT CO2e — Equivalent to 48M Cars
The GHG footprint of US food surplus equals ~48 million cars on the road annually.
Foodservice and Residential together account for the majority of emissions.
Every ton diverted from landfill yields ~3.1 MT CO2e in avoided emissions.

## Finding 8: Waste Intensity Varies Dramatically by Sector × Food Type
Produce in Farm sector shows highest waste intensity (tons wasted / tons supplied).
Fresh Meat & Seafood shows high intensity across Foodservice and Retail.
Targeting high-intensity combinations offers the best ROI for intervention programs.

## Finding 9: Surplus Has Grown 14% Since 2010 Despite Awareness
Total surplus grew from 62M (2010) to 70.7M tons (2024), a steady 14% increase.
This growth occurred despite national food waste reduction goals and campaigns.
Current voluntary approaches are insufficient — regulatory intervention is indicated.

## Policy Direction
Based on national data, highest-leverage interventions target:
- **Residential landfill waste** (11.2M tons, zero donation infrastructure) — curbside organics programs
- **Date label standardization** (spoilage + date concerns = millions of tons of preventable waste)
- **Organic waste bans** (12 states have them — what is the causal effect?)
- **Foodservice plate waste** (8.7M tons — portion standards, behavioral nudges)

## Questions for Causal Analysis
- Do organic waste bans actually reduce landfill waste?
- By how much? Does the effect grow over time?
- Which states would benefit most from adoption?
- Can we estimate the counterfactual: what would landfill tonnage be if all 50 states had bans?
